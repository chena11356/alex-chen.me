What is an

Agent-Based Model?

"A THIRD WAY OF DOING SCIENCE"

— Robert Axelrod, Professor of Political Science and Public Policy at the University of Michigan

Agent-based models (ABMs) simulate instances of individual behavior and draw conclusions on the broader system into which they are introduced. Essentially, ABMs instantiate agents in a system, introduce a degree of randomness into agent behavior, and see how agents' actions and interactions impact the system as a whole. In doing so, such models primarily aim to show that certain properties arise within the system that are not inherent in the agents themselves: in others words, they demonstrate principles of emergence.

Here are some examples of ABMs!

ABMs for Ecology ‏‏‎ ‎‏‏‎ ‎

ABMs are common in biology! For example, they can help model ecological equilibria by introducing agents of certain species of plants and animals into a simulated environment. The interactions of these agents can then help isolate the factors that may lead to the preservation or destruction of ecological stability.

The following is a simple demo ABM for ecology. Predators, denoted in red, must eat prey, denoted in blue, to survive. Prey must eat plants, denoted in green. Notice how the populations of predators, prey, and plants tend to approach an equilibrium, which can be modeled by logistic growth functions.

From this demo, it may seem that ABMs are useful for educational purposes, visualizing data in a digestible and visually appealing manner. But their primary scientific benefit comes from highlighting principles of emergence. Let's say we introduced more factors, such as conservation efforts, invasive species, or more complicated agent behavior algorithms. We could then explore how these factors impact the system as a whole, and if we input real-life ecological data, our ABM can help inform environmental policy decisions, or even monitor the health of a real ecological system!
